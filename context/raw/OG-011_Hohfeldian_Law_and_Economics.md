# OG-011: Hohfeldian Law and Economics — The FAND Approach

## 1. Purpose and Provenance

This Operational Guideline establishes how FAND uses Hohfeld's eight fundamental jural relations as the classificatory backbone of its balance sheet, and why this diverges from what practitioners of conventional law and economics (L&E) — including those trained in NUSAP-style quality assessment — would expect when they hear the phrase "law and economics." It serves as a bridging document for collaborators whose priors are Coasean/Calabresian efficiency analysis and who need to understand what FAND means by "the liability side of the balance sheet."

### 1.1 A Very American Framing

A candid admission is warranted. The argument that follows is rooted in American legal history and shaped by intellectual mentors — some of whom had a direct hand in drafting the White Plan that became the architecture of Bretton Woods. This is not an accident of biography; it reflects a structural fact about where the Hohfeldian apparatus was forged and why it matters for development measurement.

**Colonial origins of the ambiguity.** Elizabeth Mensch (1982) demonstrated that in colonial New York, the concept of a "right" to property contained within it an irreconcilable tension between two competing visions of social organization: *hierarchy* (title derived from crown sovereignty, top-down, semi-feudal, premised on divinely ordained inequality) and *voluntarism* (title derived from occupancy and use, bottom-up, republican, premised on equality and free labor). The colonial legal system could neither fully assert nor fully reject either vision. What emerged was a "safe middle ground of 'right'" — a mediating concept that selectively asserted elements of both while concealing the moral and political choices embedded in every property allocation.

Mensch showed that Adam Smith himself recognized this duality. Smith's *first* model — free exchange among self-reliant producers — was voluntarist: each colonist a republican citizen, property an extension of labor. His *second* model — concentrated capital accumulation requiring division of labor — was hierarchical: inequality protected, legal authority imposed, property as a sphere of formal title. Smith never envisioned either model in pure form; he envisioned "a complex interaction between hierarchy and voluntarism" mediated by concepts like market price, self-interest, and the "natural spectator" standard of justice.

**The Hohfeldian diagnosis.** Hohfeld's 1913 intervention was, at its core, an analytical dissolution of exactly this colonial-era ambiguity. When the word "right" is doing quadruple duty — covering claim-rights, liberties, powers, and immunities indiscriminately — every property dispute is fought on a conceptual battlefield where the contestants cannot even agree on the nature of what they hold. Hohfeld's eight atomic positions (Right/Duty, Liberty/No-right, Power/Liability, Immunity/Disability) are the "lowest common denominators of the law" (von Moschzisker 1923) — they force each usage into one of eight cells, and in doing so they make the hierarchy/voluntarism tension *analytically tractable* rather than rhetorically masked.

**A note on terminology: Liberty vs. Privilege.** Standard legal scholarship, including Kramer (2024), often treats "privilege" and "liberty" as synonymous labels for Hohfeld's Pair 2. FAND departs from this convention. A *liberty* is an inherent first-order position: the individual is free to act because no counterparty holds a claim-right against her. A *privilege*, by contrast, carries the connotation of a sovereign-granted exception — an entitlement carved out by an exercise of Power, and therefore second-order in origin even when first-order in effect. The distinction matters because it tracks the institutional dependence of the position. HR (the liberty to use one's productive capacity) is inherent where slavery is abolished; it does not require a sovereign grant. Ecosystem-service access (the privilege of using mangrove flood protection or hydropower capacity) exists within a jurisdictional framework that a sovereign Power created and could extinguish. FAND uses "Liberty" for inherent first-order freedoms and reserves "Privilege" for positions whose existence depends on a prior exercise of sovereign Power. Kramer's comprehensive treatment of the Hohfeldian positions is, of course, authoritative on the law; FAND adopts his analytical apparatus as a "thin description" — the framework most commensurate with generating testable alternatives to conventional national-accounting wisdom. Where FAND refines Kramer's terminology, it does so to serve measurement, not to contest the jurisprudence.

**Bretton Woods and the international frame.** The United States was "first among equals" at the Atlantic City Conference and at Bretton Woods (1944). The White Plan — not the Keynes Plan — shaped the architecture of the international monetary system. Joseph Gold, legendary General Counsel of the IMF (1960–1979), understood that Bretton Woods embedded not merely economic arrangements but legal architecture: the Articles of Agreement created a system of Rights (drawing rights, quota-based claims), Powers (IMF surveillance, conditionality, SDR allocation), Immunities (sovereign immunity from creditor attachment), and Duties (Article VIII obligations, par value discipline). Gold's jurisprudence was American in origin — trained in the common-law tradition where Hohfeld's framework had its deepest purchase.

When FAND proposes to classify balance-sheet entries by Hohfeldian position, it is extending a distinctly American jurisprudential tradition into development measurement. The framework has international purchase — Hohfeld has standing in EU legal scholarship, in Commonwealth common-law systems, and in international law generally — but its deepest roots are in the colonial-era struggle to define what "property" means when the sovereign, the settler, and the indigenous inhabitant all have competing but incommensurable claims.

**The FAND connection.** Mensch's hierarchy/voluntarism distinction maps directly onto Hohfeld's first-order/second-order architecture:

| Colonial vision | Hohfeldian correlate | FAND construct |
|---|---|---|
| Hierarchy (crown-granted title, formal right, enforceable obligation) | First-order Right/Duty | PA, NC, CBA/CBL |
| Voluntarism (occupancy, use, labor, community membership) | First-order Liberty/No-right | HR (where slavery is abolished) |
| Crown prerogative (power to grant, revoke, rezone, privilege) | Second-order Power/Liability | SR (sovereign regulatory capacity); Privilege = sovereign-granted access (ES) |
| Constitutional constraint (limits on crown/sovereign) | Second-order Immunity/Disability | SI (institutional quality), Takings Clause equivalents |

What Mensch showed as the historical origin of the ambiguity, Hohfeld formalized as a classificatory discipline, and FAND operationalizes as a balance-sheet architecture. The "safe middle ground of 'right'" that colonial lawyers created is precisely what SNA 2008 still occupies — recognizing only the Right/Duty pair and leaving the rest as implicit omissions or residuals.

## 2. Conventional L&E Priors and Their Extensions

A scholar or practitioner coming to FAND from mainstream L&E carries a mental model that has evolved through three distinct phases. FAND engages — and then departs from — each of them.

### 2.1 The Coase-Calabresi Frame (1960–1972)

Property is a "bundle of sticks" — a collection of use-permissions allocated by courts or regulators to maximize social welfare. The central question is allocative: given incompatible uses A and B, who should bear the cost? Coase (1960) showed that in a zero-transaction-cost world the initial allocation is irrelevant; Calabresi and Melamed (1972) showed that in the real world the choice between property rules and liability rules determines who the "cheapest cost avoider" is and whether to protect entitlements by injunction (property rule) or by damages (liability rule).

**The Posner-Demsetz extension.** Property rights are "collectively imposed allocation[s] of use rights" (Merrill and Smith 2001 characterizing this view). The economist takes the bundle as datum and asks what forces determine the price. The legal analyst asks which reallocation of sticks maximizes efficiency.

**What this frame does NOT do:**

- It does not distinguish between Rights and Privileges in Hohfeld's sense. The word "right" in Coase/Posner covers claim-rights, liberties, powers, and immunities indiscriminately — precisely the ambiguity Hohfeld (1913) diagnosed.
- It does not distinguish first-order from second-order positions. A regulatory power to rezone land and a farmer's right to exclude trespassers are both "sticks" in the bundle, despite being categorically different jural relations.
- It does not systematically address the liability side of the balance sheet as a set of jural positions. Liabilities in Coase-world are costs to be minimized, not jural correlates to be classified.

**The NUSAP implication.** A NUSAP practitioner assessing the pedigree of a claim about "property rights" would find that the conventional L&E frame operates at a high level of linguistic ambiguity (spread on the S-axis) precisely because the word "right" is doing quadruple duty. Hohfeld's contribution is to collapse that spread by forcing each usage into one of eight atomic positions.

### 2.2 Hart, Williamson, and Incomplete Contracts (1986–2000)

Oliver Hart and the Grossman-Hart-Moore (GHM) tradition introduced a fundamentally different question: when contracts are *incomplete* — when not all contingencies can be specified ex ante — who decides what happens in the unspecified cases? Their answer: the owner of the asset, defined as the holder of *residual control rights*.

**Residual control rights are Hohfeldian Power.** Hart's "residual control rights" map precisely to Hohfeld's second-order Power — the capacity to alter legal relations in unspecified contingencies. The asset owner, in GHM theory, is the party who decides how the asset is used when the contract is silent. This is not a first-order Right (no specific Duty-bearer exists for the unspecified contingency). It is a Power: the capacity to change the jural landscape by making a decision that others must accommodate.

**What Hart sees:**
- Ownership = residual control rights = Power over the asset in uncontracted states
- Efficiency depends on allocating control rights to the party whose investment is most relationship-specific
- Transaction costs make complete contracting impossible → residual control rights are the residual determinant of efficiency

**What Hart neglects — the correlatives:**

This is the critical point for FAND. Hart's framework focuses exclusively on the *asset side* of the Power relation — who holds the residual control right. It does not systematically examine the *correlative positions* that Hohfeld's framework requires:

| Position Hart analyzes | Hohfeldian correlative Hart neglects | Why it is neglected |
|---|---|---|
| Power (residual control right) | **Liability** — susceptibility of others to having their jural position altered by the Power-holder's decision | Not measured because no contracting framework captures what happens to parties affected by unspecified exercises of Power |
| — | **Disability** — absence of Power in parties who cannot alter the control-right holder's position | Not measured because the Substantive Rights (Immunities) they correlate with are themselves unmeasured |

The neglect is not accidental. It is structural. Hart cannot measure the correlative Liability because it would require measuring what Hohfeld called "susceptibility to alteration" — the exposure of counterparties to decisions they cannot anticipate and cannot contract against. And he cannot measure Disability because it is the correlative of Immunity — protections whose existence depends on institutional quality that no economic theory endogenizes.

**Williamson's governance structures.** Oliver Williamson's transaction-cost economics (1985, 1996) extends the incomplete-contracts framework to governance: hierarchies, markets, and hybrid forms are alternative structures for managing the residual uncertainty that GHM identifies. In Hohfeldian terms, Williamson is asking: what institutional architecture converts raw Power/Liability into structured Power-with-Immunity-constraints? The answer — courts, regulatory agencies, relational contracts, reputation mechanisms — is precisely what FAND measures as SI (Social Infrastructure): the aggregate of second-order positions that determine whether Power is exercised within or outside Disability constraints.

### 2.3 Agency Costs and the Sovereignty Problem

Jensen and Meckling (1976) and the agency-cost tradition identify a further refinement: when the Power-holder's interests diverge from the interests of those bearing the correlative Liability, the exercise of Power becomes extractive rather than productive. In the corporate context, this is the principal-agent problem. In the sovereign context — which is FAND's domain — it is the problem of government failure.

**The FAND claim:** An entity co-opting residual control rights is exercising sovereignty — Power and/or Privilege. If the co-optation is systemic, it represents a failure of government, *if the governed are sovereign*. This is the connection between Hart's "who holds the residual control right?" and FAND's "who/what is sovereign?" (the annotation John places under the Liability/Disability columns in the Book1 matrix).

The specific mechanism: when private entities (banks, corporations, extractive interests) accumulate Powers that should be constrained by Disabilities, the Liability falls on the public — susceptibility to having fiscal, environmental, or social positions altered by private exercises of sovereign-grade Power. FAND's Financial System Overhang (1.2.2.2) is the stock measure of this co-optation: the implicit Duty of the sovereign to backstop financial system losses. In Hohfeldian terms, the public bears a Liability (susceptibility to fiscal alteration by the banking system's too-big-to-fail Power) that should be constrained by Disability (regulatory architecture) but is not — because the Disabilities have been eroded by the very entities exercising the Power.

## 3. What FAND Does Differently: The Hohfeldian Prior

FAND's balance sheet classifies every entry by its Hohfeldian position *before* asking about its SNA treatment, its data source, or its valuation method. This is documented in SPMF-214-2 (`.claude/reference/hohfeld-jural-relations.md`) and the balance-sheet-identity rule. The key structural claims:

### 3.1 Asset Side — Largely Established

The asset side maps cleanly onto first-order and second-order positions:

| FAND construct | Hohfeldian position | SNA coverage |
|---|---|---|
| PA (Produced Assets) | First-order Right/Duty | Full |
| NC (Natural Capital) | First-order Right/Duty | Partial (market rents only) |
| CBA (Cross-Border Assets) | First-order Right/Duty, cross-border | Full (IMF IIP) |
| HR (Human Resources) | First-order Liberty/No-right. What code 1.1.2.1 sits under Substantive Rights (1.1.2), not Claim-Rights (1.1.1). The Liberty exists where and when slavery is abolished (Fisher 1906, Hicks 1939). | None |
| SR (Other Substantive Rights) | Second-order Power/Immunity + Privilege/No-right (sovereign-granted access) | None |

The analytical frontier is everything SNA excludes: the Liberty/No-right pair (HR), the Privilege/No-right pair (ecosystem services as sovereign-granted access), and the second-order row (institutional quality, sovereign regulatory capacity).

### 3.2 Liability Side — The Frontier This Guideline Addresses

The liability side of the balance sheet is where FAND's Hohfeldian approach becomes genuinely novel — and genuinely fuzzier. The conventional L&E practitioner expects liabilities to be costs. FAND treats them as jural positions held by *counterparties* to the asset-side positions.

**The liability side of the Hohfeldian table:**

```
Asset-side position → Liability-side correlate
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
Right (A holds)      → Duty (B bears)
Liberty (A holds)    → No-right (B bears — cannot compel; inherent)
Privilege (A holds)  → No-right (B bears — cannot compel; sovereign-granted)
Power (A holds)      → Liability (B is susceptible to alteration)
Immunity (A holds)   → Disability (B cannot alter A's position)
```

Each correlate generates a distinct type of balance-sheet liability:

#### 3.2.1 Duty-Side Liabilities (Correlative of Rights)

These are the only liabilities that conventional national accounts recognize. When A has a Right, B has a Duty — a legally enforceable obligation.

**Well-measured examples in FAND:**
- **CBL (Cross-Border Liabilities, 1.2.3)**: Gross foreign liabilities. B = domestic territory; A = foreign claim-right holders. Directly observed via IMF IIP. Five Hohfeldian-institutional children (Via-DFS, Sovereign Debt, Nonbank-Banks, Inward FDI, All-Other). HIGH pedigree.
- **DM (Deferred Maintenance, 1.2.1.1)**: The trust-infrastructure deficit. Formally: max(0, 0.20 × CW − SI). Not a Duty in the strict legal sense but a liability-by-identity — the balance-sheet gap that arises when SI falls below the structural floor. MEDIUM pedigree (derived, not observed).

**Fuzzier Duty-side liabilities:**
- **Unfunded Social Security (1.2.2.1)**: The federal government's Duty to pay future benefits. The correlative Right-holder is the beneficiary population. The obligation quantum is defined by national statute (OASDI Trust Fund actuarial projections). *Flemming v. Nestor* (1960) established that SSA benefits are not contractual rights — Congress can alter them. In Hohfeldian terms, the beneficiary holds something between a Right and a Privilege: the Duty is politically binding but not court-compellable in the same way as sovereign debt service. NUSAP pedigree assessment: HIGH spread (legal status genuinely ambiguous), MEDIUM strength (actuarial projections exist but depend on demographic/fiscal assumptions stretching 75 years).
- **Financial System Overhang (1.2.2.2)**: The stock measure of co-opted sovereignty. No statute defines the sovereign's obligation to backstop financial system losses; it is revealed ex post in crises. Hohfeldian classification: the public bears a *Liability* (susceptibility to having its fiscal position altered by a financial Power exercised by the banking system's too-big-to-fail dynamic). This is second-order, not first-order — it is not a Duty to pay a fixed sum but a susceptibility to having one's legal-fiscal relations altered by private exercise of sovereign-grade Power (see §2.3). NUSAP pedigree: VERY HIGH spread, LOW strength.

#### 3.2.2 No-Right-Side Positions (Correlative of Liberties and Privileges)

This is the most conceptually challenging liability-side category. When A holds a Liberty or Privilege — freedom to act without any counterparty able to compel — B holds a No-right. B cannot claim anything from A.

**What does a No-right look like on a balance sheet?**

Consider HR (Human Resources). HR sits at What code 1.1.2.1, under Substantive Rights (1.1.2) — not under Claim-Rights (1.1.1). The hierarchy confirms the classification: HR is a first-order Liberty/No-right. The individual's liberty to use her productive capacity is inherent — it does not require a sovereign grant — and the correlative No-right means no counterparty can compel the labor.

**The slavery caveat.** This classification carries a condition that Fisher (1906), Hicks (1939), and every careful economist since has noted: the Liberty exists *where and when slavery is abolished*. Under slavery, human productive capacity is a Right/Duty pair — the slave-owner holds the claim-right, the slave bears the Duty. Abolition converts Right/Duty into Liberty/No-right: the individual is free, no one can compel the labor. But that conversion required an exercise of sovereign Power (the 13th Amendment in the US case) creating an Immunity (constitutional prohibition of involuntary servitude). The Liberty is first-order in its effect but second-order in its establishment — it took an institutional act to bring it into being.

This is not merely historical. Dray et al. (World Bank) have recently demonstrated that the path-dependence consequences of slavery persist in measurable form — territories where the institutional conversion from Right/Duty to Liberty/No-right was incomplete or contested continue to exhibit lower exercisability of the formal Liberty. The Liberty is established on paper, but the Immunity that makes it real (access to education, health, labor-market freedom, rule of law) is unevenly distributed. In FAND terms, these are territories where DM is high: the institutional architecture has not fully converted the formal Liberty into exercisable productive capacity. The DM measure quantifies exactly this gap — the distance between the Liberty as formally granted and the Immunity infrastructure needed to make it substantively exercisable.

In Mensch's colonial terms, the Liberty reading corresponds to the *voluntarist* vision: labor as the extension of free will, property as occupancy and use, title derived from the act of working the land rather than from sovereign grant. The individual's liberty to use her productive capacity does not depend on any counterparty's Duty. The correlative No-right means: *no counterparty can compel the labor*. This is not a liability in the financial sense — it is an *absence of claim*. But it has balance-sheet consequences:

- A territory where HR is large relative to PA+NC is one where most wealth resides in positions that *no one can claim against*. The territory's "liability" is that it cannot collateralize this wealth — it cannot force its population to remain, to work, to be productive. Brain drain is the realization of the No-right position: the territory had no claim-right over the departing human capital.
- In the FAND balance sheet, this manifests as the gap between Comprehensive Wealth (which includes HR) and Net Worth as conventionally measured (which excludes it). The No-right is not a line item; it is the *structural reason* why HR cannot appear as an asset in SNA.

**The NUSAP bridge:** A No-right position has inherently low pedigree because it is defined by *absence*. You cannot observe what no one can claim. You can only infer it from the Liberty-side measurement (the HR regression) and note that the correlative is structural uncollateralizability. This is precisely the kind of construct that NUSAP's "border with ignorance" category (Funtowicz and Ravetz 1990) is designed to handle.

**The "CB Impaired?" annotation.** John's Book1 matrix annotates the No-right column with "CB Impaired?" — asking whether the central bank's position is impaired when it faces No-right positions rather than claim-rights. The answer is structural: a central bank can collateralize Rights (sovereign debt, financial claims) but cannot collateralize Liberties or Privileges (human productive capacity, ecosystem services). When a territory's wealth composition shifts from Rights toward Liberties — from PA/NC toward HR/ES — the central bank's operational capacity to intermediate diminishes. The territory's wealth is real but uncollateralizable. This is the deep structural reason why DM (Deferred Maintenance) maps onto the intermediation gap: nations where DM is large are those where the wealth-composition is Liberty-heavy but the institutional infrastructure to convert Liberties into exercisable productive capacity (education, health, rule of law) is thin.

#### 3.2.3 Liability-Side Positions (Correlative of Powers) and the Hart Connection

When A holds a Power — capacity to alter legal relations — B holds a Liability in Hohfeld's technical sense: susceptibility to having one's jural position changed. This is where GHM meets Hohfeld most directly.

Hart's "residual control rights" = Power. The party whose investment is not protected by complete contracts is exposed to Liability — susceptibility to having her returns altered by the Power-holder's decisions. Hart asks: allocate Power to maximize joint surplus. FAND asks: what happens to the counterparties bearing the correlative Liability, and who monitors the Disabilities that should constrain the Power?

**FAND applications:**

- **Sovereign regulatory risk**: A territory's Power to rezone, nationalize, or impose windfall taxes means that asset-holders (domestic and foreign) bear a Liability — their Rights can be extinguished or diminished by sovereign action. This is not priced in SNA at all. In financial markets it appears as "political risk premium" or "sovereign spread." FAND's cross-border architecture partially captures this: the Hohfeldian-institutional children of CBL (especially Sovereign Debt and Inward FDI) reflect *how* the sovereign exercises its Power over foreign claimants.
- **Constitutional immunity constraints**: The US Takings Clause (5th Amendment) converts some Liabilities into Immunities — the sovereign's Power to take property is constrained by a Duty to compensate. Harrison (2017) analyzes this: the Ninth Amendment protects legal interests *not created by the Constitution* by limiting federal Power, thereby converting what would be Liability positions into Immunity positions. The balance-sheet implication: territories with stronger constitutional protections have lower effective Liability on the Power/Liability channel, which should manifest as higher asset values (lower political risk discount).
- **Co-opted sovereignty (Financial System Overhang)**: When private entities accumulate Powers that should be Disability-constrained — when banks become too big to fail, when extractive interests capture regulatory agencies, when corporate lobbying converts public Immunities into private Powers — the Liability falls on the public fisc. The co-optation is measurable in principle (Fed Z.1 financial sector negative net worth) and is the stock counterpart of the flow externality that Hart's framework cannot capture because it focuses on the Power-holder, not the Liability-bearer.

#### 3.2.4 Disability-Side Positions (Correlative of Immunities) and the Self-Reference Problem

When A holds an Immunity — protection from external Power — B holds a Disability: absence of power to alter A's position.

**FAND applications:**

- **SI (Savings & Institutional Quality)**: SI is the aggregate of second-order positions. High SI means the territory's institutional architecture creates Immunities — property rights are protected from arbitrary Power, contracts are enforced, regulatory takings are compensated. The correlative Disability falls on potential Power-wielders (including the sovereign itself when bound by rule of law). When SI is high, the Disability channel is strong: those who might alter legal relations *cannot*. This is the "trust" that makes collateralization possible.
- **The DM/SI threshold**: The 20% CW floor for SI (SR = max(SI, 0.20 × CW)) is, in Hohfeldian terms, a prior that *every territory has some positive Immunity/Disability structure*. Even failed states have residual institutional capital. The DM liability (max(0, 0.20 × CW − SI)) quantifies the gap between actual and floor-level Disability structure. In advocacy terms, DM quantifies the aggregate Disability deficit — the extent to which Power-wielders are *not* constrained by the institutional architecture that should Disable them.

**The self-reference problem.** The sovereign occupies all four Hohfeldian positions simultaneously:

| Position | Sovereign role |
|---|---|
| Power | Source of regulatory, legislative, and judicial authority |
| Disability | Self-created: constitutional constraints, rule of law, treaty obligations |
| Liability | Susceptibility to its own institutional architecture (the sovereign can alter even the rules that constrain it) |
| Immunity | Protection from external Powers (sovereignty in international law) |

This circularity — the sovereign is simultaneously the source of Power, the creator of Disabilities, the bearer of Liability, and the holder of Immunity — is not a conceptual deficiency. It is the structural reason *why* SI is measured as a residual. You cannot directly observe the self-referential loop of institutional constraint. You can only observe its *effects*: the gap between what the income annuity implies should exist (CW) and what claim-rights can account for (PA + NC + HR). SI = CW − PA − NC − HR is the residual trace of the self-referential sovereign — and its decomposition into SR (what the Immunities preserve) and DM (what the absent Disabilities cost) is the balance-sheet expression of Mensch's colonial question: is this a territory where hierarchy serves the governed (high SI, low DM), or one where hierarchy extracts from the governed (low SI, high DM)?

John's Book1 annotation — "Who/What is Sovereign?" under the Liability/Disability columns — is the deepest question in the framework. The Hohfeldian analysis does not answer it. It makes the question *tractable* by decomposing it into measurable positions with explicit pedigree assessments.

## 4. The FAND Departure: Why This Is Not Coasean L&E

The Coase-Calabresi frame asks: "Given incompatible uses, who should bear the cost?" This is a first-order Right/Duty question, and it assumes the answer is a reallocation of the Right/Duty pair.

Hart-GHM asks: "Given incomplete contracts, who should hold residual control rights?" This is a second-order Power question, but it examines only the Power side — not the correlative Liability, not the constraining Disability.

FAND asks a different question at each jural level:

| Jural level | FAND question | Coasean equivalent | GHM equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| Right/Duty | Who holds the claim-right and who bears the duty? | Who gets the entitlement? | What is contractible? |
| Liberty/No-right | What wealth is structurally uncollateralizable? | *(not asked)* | *(not asked)* |
| Power/Liability | What is the sovereign's capacity to alter the entitlement structure, and who bears the exposure? | *(implicit in "liability rules" but not separately measured)* | Who holds residual control rights? *(correlative Liability not measured)* |
| Immunity/Disability | What constrains the sovereign's power, and at what cost to whom? | *(not asked)* | *(not asked — the institutional architecture is exogenous)* |

The conventional L&E practitioner collapses all four levels into "property rights vs. liability rules." Hart collapses the second-order row into "who has control?" FAND keeps all four levels and both sides (position and correlative) separate because they produce different balance-sheet entries with different pedigrees, different data sources, and different policy implications.

**The Long (2013) insight** ("Waiting for Hohfeld"): the rhetorical collapse of Liberty into Right has physical consequences — when all property interests are called "rights," the community's political capacity to regulate diminishes because the frame assumes a Duty-bearer who must be compensated. If the interest is actually a Liberty, there is no Duty-bearer, no compensation obligation, and the regulatory question is entirely different. Long's argument applies with equal force to the collapse of Privilege into Liberty: a Privilege (sovereign-granted access) can be revoked by the Power that created it; a Liberty (inherent freedom) cannot be revoked without creating a new Duty (i.e., re-establishing slavery or servitude). The distinction determines whether the sovereign is *granting* something it could withdraw (Privilege) or *recognizing* something that exists independently (Liberty). FAND's NC/SR distinction (BT-021) implements the Privilege side: hydropower and mangroves were relocated from NC (Right/Duty) to SR (Privilege/No-right) because the flood protection mangroves provide is not *owed* by any party — it is a sovereign-granted use-access that could, in principle, be extinguished by the sovereign Power that permits it. Misclassifying it as a Right would imply a Duty-bearer who doesn't exist; misclassifying it as a Liberty would imply an inherent freedom that doesn't require institutional maintenance.

**The Claeys (2011) corrective** ("Bundle of Sticks"): the bundle-of-sticks metaphor, which Coase and Demsetz naturalized, treats property as a collection of permissions. Claeys argues that property is better understood as a *domain of authority* — structured, not atomic. FAND follows Claeys: the balance sheet is not a list of sticks but a structured hierarchy of jural positions, with first-order positions (Rights, Privileges) nested under second-order positions (Powers, Immunities) that determine how the first-order positions can be altered.

## 5. The Liability-Side Measurement Challenge

The core difficulty your NUSAP contacts would immediately recognize: **liability-side Hohfeldian correlates decrease in measurability as you move from Duty toward Disability.**

```
PEDIGREE GRADIENT (liability side):
                                                          
  Duty ──────── No-right ──────── Liability ──────── Disability
  │                │                  │                  │
  High             Very Low           Low-Medium         Very Low
  (observed:       (absence of        (revealed in       (inferred from
   debt, IIP,       claim;             crises;            institutional
   SSA actuarial)   structural         political risk     quality
                    inference)         premiums)          indices)
```

This gradient is not a deficiency — it is an inherent property of the jural structure. First-order Duties are observable because they are legally enforceable and generate cash flows. No-rights are absences. Second-order Liabilities are contingent. Disabilities are constraints on power that manifest only when power is *not* exercised.

**NUSAP numeral-unit-spread-assessment-pedigree mapping:**

| Correlate | Numeral (typical) | Spread | Pedigree |
|---|---|---|---|
| Duty (CBL, Sovereign debt) | Stock in USD, observed | Narrow | Tier-A (IMF IIP) |
| Duty (Unfunded SSA) | PV of actuarial projections | Moderate | Tier-C (model-dependent, 75-year horizon) |
| No-right (HR uncollateralizability) | Not directly measurable | Very wide | Tier-E (structural inference from Liberty-side) |
| Liability (regulatory/sovereign risk) | Basis-point spreads, event-conditional | Wide | Tier-D (market-implied, crisis-revealed) |
| Disability (institutional constraints on power) | Index-based (WGI, rule-of-law scores) | Wide | Tier-D (survey-based, subjective) |

The FAND balance sheet does not pretend that all liability-side entries have equal pedigree. The hierarchy accommodates this:
- **1.2.1 (Intragenerational)** = DM. Identity-derived. The spread is inherited from the SI regression.
- **1.2.2 (Intergenerational)** = jurisdiction-specific first-order Duties. Bottom-up only. No cross-country comparability (this is the tl1-vs-fips-duality rule).
- **1.2.3 (Cross-Border Duties)** = directly observed. Highest pedigree on the liability side.

The No-right, Liability, and Disability channels do not yet have dedicated balance-sheet nodes. They operate as *explanatory infrastructure* — they explain WHY certain measured quantities behave as they do (why HR can't be collateralized, why political risk discounts vary, why SI tracks institutional quality indices). Whether they should be promoted to measured nodes with explicit What-codes is a future architectural question.

## 6. Operational Rules

1. **Classification before measurement.** When a new liability-side candidate is proposed, determine its Hohfeldian correlate first. Is it a Duty (enforceable obligation)? A No-right (absence of claim)? A Liability (susceptibility to Power)? A Disability (constraint on power)? The answer determines which branch of 1.2.* it enters, what data sources are admissible, and what pedigree tier it starts at.

2. **No Coasean collapsing.** Do not merge Right/Duty and Power/Liability into a single "entitlement/cost" frame. The Calabresi-Melamed distinction between property rules and liability rules is a special case of the first-order/second-order distinction, not a substitute for it.

3. **No Hart truncation.** Do not analyze Power without its correlative Liability and constraining Disability. Hart's "residual control rights" framework is valuable for identifying *who holds Power*; FAND extends it by asking *who bears the exposure* and *what constrains the Power-holder*. The correlatives are where the measurement frontier lies.

4. **Liberty/No-right and Privilege/No-right are real.** The absence of a Duty-bearer is not a deficiency in the data — it is the correct classification of positions where no enforceable obligation exists. HR is Liberty/No-right (inherent, where slavery is abolished). Ecosystem services are Privilege/No-right (sovereign-granted access). Both produce No-right correlatives, but they differ in institutional dependence: a Liberty cannot be revoked without creating a new Duty; a Privilege can be withdrawn by the Power that granted it. Attempting to find a Duty-bearer where none exists is the category error that led CWoN to expand NC to include mangroves and hydropower.

5. **Liability-side pedigree is inherently lower.** Accept this. NUSAP's framework is designed precisely for situations where the pedigree gradient is steep. The appropriate response is not to exclude low-pedigree positions but to report them with explicit spread and assessment metadata.

6. **Intergenerational liabilities are jurisdiction-specific Duties.** They cannot be built top-down from internationally harmonized sources because the correlative Right-holders and obligation quanta are defined by national law. The tl1-vs-fips-duality rule implements this: 1.2.2 children exist only in the FIPS version of the balance sheet, not the ISO3/TL1 version.

7. **Second-order liability positions (Liability, Disability) are currently explanatory, not measured.** They enter the analytical narrative — why SI matters, why DM is a priority, why political risk discounts vary — but do not yet have What-codes. Promotion to measured nodes requires a data-source with at least Tier-D pedigree and a clear Hohfeldian classification.

8. **Co-opted sovereignty is measurable in principle.** Financial System Overhang (1.2.2.2) is the first attempt to quantify what happens when private entities exercise sovereign-grade Power without sovereign-grade Disability constraints. Future nodes may extend this to regulatory capture, extractive-industry concession terms, and other domains where the Power/Liability balance has shifted.

## 9. The Bridge to NUSAP Interlocutors

When presenting FAND's Hohfeldian approach to scholars familiar with NUSAP but expecting conventional L&E:

**Start here:** "We agree with Coase that the allocation of entitlements matters, and with Hart that residual control rights are decisive when contracts are incomplete. But we think both frameworks are examining only one side of Hohfeld's table. Coase sees Rights and costs. Hart sees Powers and efficiency. Neither examines the correlatives — the Duties, No-rights, Liabilities, and Disabilities that fall on the counterparties. FAND's balance sheet keeps both sides because the correlatives are where measurement quality degrades and where policy leverage actually resides."

**Then the colonial framing:** "This is, we acknowledge, a very American approach. The ambiguity Hohfeld diagnosed in 1913 was baked into American property law from its colonial origins — Mensch showed it was the irreconcilable tension between sovereignty-derived title and labor-derived title. Bretton Woods exported the same ambiguity into the international system. FAND proposes to make it analytically tractable rather than rhetorically masked."

**Then the asset side:** "On the asset side, the distinction between Rights (claim-rights: PA, NC), Liberties (inherent freedoms: HR, where slavery is abolished), and Privileges (sovereign-granted access: ecosystem services) determines what SNA can see and what it misses. Fisher and Hicks conditioned human capital on abolition; Dray et al. have shown the path-dependence consequences persist. This is our established analytical frontier."

**Then the new territory:** "On the liability side, we're proposing that the jural correlates — Duty, No-right, Liability, Disability — generate a pedigree gradient that maps directly onto NUSAP's quality framework. The highest-pedigree liabilities are first-order Duties (sovereign debt, IIP positions). The fuzziest are second-order Disabilities (constraints on sovereign power, measured only by institutional quality indices). We don't pretend these have equal reliability. We propose that the Hohfeldian structure *is* the pedigree map — it tells you where the measurement frontier is and why."

**The Hart extension:** "Hart showed that when contracts are incomplete, residual control rights — Power in Hohfeld's terms — determine efficiency. We extend this: the correlative Liability (who bears the exposure when Power is exercised) and the constraining Disability (what prevents the Power-holder from extracting) are the unmeasured costs of incomplete governance. When those costs are systemic — when private entities co-opt sovereign Power without bearing the correlative Disability constraints — you get Financial System Overhang, regulatory capture, and the measurement frontier that FAND's DM quantifies."

**The payoff:** "Conventional L&E asks 'who should bear the cost.' Hart asks 'who should hold the control right.' We ask 'what is the jural position of every party in the relation, and what does the full Hohfeldian table tell us about measurement reliability, institutional prerequisites, and policy leverage.' A Duty can be restructured by negotiation. A Liability can be triggered by sovereign action. A Disability constrains the sovereign itself. These are not interchangeable — and treating them as a single 'cost' or a single 'control right' is exactly the kind of ambiguity that Hohfeld, Mensch, and NUSAP were each, in their own domains, designed to dissolve."
