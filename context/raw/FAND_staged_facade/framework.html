The Framework

A country, read like a company that answers to its owners

Companies answer to their owners with a balance sheet — what they own, what they owe, what's left over. FAND does the same for a country. The twist is who the owners are: not shareholders, but the governed.

Production the worked example on this page is Production-grade; every subnational figure keeps its own badge.

Read it like a 10-K — three layers, one number

Theodore Porter, the historian of measurement whose work shaped the original 1995 wealth accounts, called this a thin description: an honest surface that earns trust only if the commensurate detail is there to back it when you ask. FAND is built as three nested layers, the way a corporate 10-K is:

  1. The balance sheet — one page, one bottom line: Net Worth. What the nation owns, what it owes, and the trust infrastructure in between.
  2. The items — drill into any line and it opens: produced assets, natural capital, human resources, substantive rights.
  3. The transactions — drill again and you reach the flows that changed the items over time — the income-statement side.

The design goal, in John's phrase, is "minimizing how many Whys get to Oh": a reader should be able to keep asking why is that number what it is? and keep getting an answer, until the why resolves to an oh — I see. Nothing bottoms out in "trust us."

The two branches: a photograph and a video

The split between "items" and "transactions" is the framework's highest-order sorting decision, and it has a simple test — the photograph test:

  • A stock is something you could photograph at an instant: a building, a forest, a bank balance, a population count. Stocks live in the Portfolio branch — the balance sheet.
  • A flow is an activity that only makes sense over a span of time: production, spending, income, service delivery — a video, not a photograph. Flows live in the Wheel of Wealth branch — the income statement.

Avatars. A few balance-sheet items can't literally be photographed but still have to be modeled — Human Resources is the clearest case. These modeled stocks are called avatars, and they are flagged as such, openly, so a reader always knows which numbers are observed and which are inferred.

A communication system, not a report

Here is where FAND stops being a 10-K. A corporate report is a one-way document: management reports to the owners. FAND is not a report. It is a communication system between them — and the roles are the ones the American founders assigned. Management is government. The owners are the governed — everyone who lives in a place or holds a claim on it.

That single reframing is the whole reason the site is built from the bottom up. A company's owners are handed a finished statement. A nation's owners are supposed to be the source of the values on it. So the aggregate balance sheet you meet at the top of the site is not a verdict handed down — it is built to become a rollup of what the owners value: ST = Σ(CT), the sum of its parts, extended all the way down.

Recognize before you value — and why that needs AI

The framework performs two operations on anything people care about, and it keeps them strictly separate:

  • Recognize. Acknowledge that something exists and matters enough that someone, somewhere has spent effort measuring or arguing about it. Recognition is deliberately generous.
  • Value. Assign it a worth. Valuation is contested by nature — and those disagreements are themselves information.

Conventional statistics collapse these two steps, and in doing so they quietly let the measurement decide the values — a trap the philosopher C. Thi Nguyen calls value capture. FAND refuses the collapse: recognition is expansive; valuation is deferred to the people whose values they are. A thing recognized but not yet priced is not worth zero — it is recognized and awaiting judgment, which is a completely different statement. Reversing the 1890 Hollerith Pivot is the entire reason the instrument is built with agentic AI.

"Valuing at zero is not caution — it is an assertion" (OG-001)
By recognizing only market claim-rights, orthodox national accounting "does not merely omit the other entitlements — it effectively values them at zero. A person's liberty to spend time with family, their power to participate in governance, their immunity from arbitrary interference: these are real positions … and they consume the same finite resource — time — as any employment contract. Valuing them at zero is not caution; it is an assertion that systematically distorts every allocation decision built on the accounts."

Two mechanisms make this concrete: commensuration (Espeland & Stevens) renders some things visible and comparable while making everything it can't price invisible; value capture (Nguyen) is what happens next, once life is reduced to the shared scale. FAND's defense is structural: recognition is comprehensive and valuation is deferred, so the classification scheme never decides what things are worth.

  • PA — net stock of produced fixed assets: OECD balance-sheet data extended to ~210 countries by a perpetual-inventory model.
  • NC — only severable claim-rights (fuels, minerals, timber, farmland, fisheries). Ecosystem services like mangrove flood protection and hydropower are not here — they sit under Substantive Rights, because no counterparty has a duty to provide them. Valued by a three-halo method, never imported from CWoN.
  • HR — capitalized productive value of people, recovered by a single regression of residual wealth on mean years of schooling and life-expectancy-capped-at-65, fit once at the global level and applied unchanged at every scale (Spec D-cap: α=4.748, β_MYS=0.293, β_LE=0.072, β_t=−0.674; R²=0.497, N=10,031).
  • SI / SR / DM — Social Infrastructure is the honest residual, SI = CW − PA − NC − HR, split against a structural floor of 20% of Commonwealth: SR = max(SI, 0.20·CW) is the asset side, DM = max(0, 0.20·CW − SI) the liability side (binds in 96 of 194 countries in 2020). The 20% is a structural prior, not an estimate — meant to be displaced by local evidence.
  • CBA / CBL — foreign claim-rights held and duties owed, from IMF international-investment-position data.

Why GDP can't see the biggest number — Hohfeld's missing columns

In 1913 Wesley Hohfeld showed that every legal relationship is one of eight positions in four correlative pairs — Right/Duty, Liberty/No-right, Power/Liability, Immunity/Disability. The System of National Accounts recognizes only the first pair: claim-rights and their duties. Everything else a society holds — the liberty to use one's own time, the power to participate in governance, the immunity from arbitrary interference — falls outside the accounting boundary and is therefore, silently, valued at nothing. FAND's entire analytical frontier is exactly the material SNA leaves out.

SNA's choice to operationalize only Right/Duty reflected administrative feasibility in the 1950s, not conceptual completeness — the punch card couldn't read the other columns, so they were dropped. Hohfeld's framework predates that choice by four decades.

From residual to recognized — the base-primary mechanism

Social Infrastructure is a residual in the current build — the framework is honest that this is the surgeon's-triage stopgap, not the destination. The destination is de-residualization: recognizing and scoring the components of trust infrastructure directly. FAND does not compute what each component is worth. It fixes the credible dollar totals (the 20% floor) and seeds a defensible, open list of components; then a geolocated consensus process among the governed allocates the total among the components. FAND computes the thin dollar envelope; the owners supply the thick allocation within it.
