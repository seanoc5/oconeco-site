1. What OconEco Does (30-Second Version)

GDP tells you what a country earns in a year. We measure what it owns — its full balance sheet. That includes factories and roads, but also oil reserves, forests, the value of an educated population, and the institutional trust that makes a society work.

We do this for 200+ countries (back to 1950), 50 US states, and 3,258 US counties. Nobody else does this at this scale or with this level of detail.

The website makes these results visible to institutional peers (World Bank alumni, academic reviewers, development agencies) and eventually to anyone interested in how places build or lose wealth over time.

2. The Core Concept — A Balance Sheet for Places

Think of a company's balance sheet: assets on one side, liabilities on the other, net worth at the bottom. We do the same thing for countries, states, and counties. This is the central object the entire website presents.

Balance Sheet of a Territory (Country, State, or County)
1.1  Assets
Claim-Rights — things you can buy, sell, borrow against
PA — Produced Assets (factories, roads, buildings)
NC — Natural Capital (oil, minerals, timber, fish, farmland)
CBA — Cross-Border Assets (claims on Commonwealth elsewhere)
Substantive Rights — valuable but not collateralizable
HR — Human Resources (education and health endowments)
SR — Other Substantive Rights (ecosystem services, institutional trust)
1.2  Liabilities  (to whom?)
Cross-Border Duties — recognized rights of others to a place's wealth
CBL — Foreign liabilities, inter-state obligations, commuter outflows
Deferred Maintenance — the trust-infrastructure deficit
DM — The gap closed by investing more in how people collaborate and compete (strengthening communities, institutions, rule of law).
1.2.4  Net Worth  =  Assets – CBL – DM
Liability to the owners of the place.  Whose net worth is it?
Two key insights you need to understand: (1) Most of the world's wealth is in the substantive rights column — education, health, institutional trust. These are things you can't sell on a market or borrow against at a bank, but they're what actually makes a place prosperous. Nobody else measures them. We do. That's why this project exists. (2) Net Worth is a liability — what the entity owes its owners. For a corporation, owners are shareholders. For a place, the question is: whose net worth is it? The governed. That question — who owns the wealth of a place and how well is it being managed — is what the entire site must make visible.

What changes as you zoom in: the cross-border story

The balance sheet equation is the same at every level, but "cross-border" means something completely different depending on the scale — and the enforceability of claims varies even within the same nominal legal system. A contract enforceable in Manhattan may face materially different enforcement in rural Mississippi. This is one of the most interesting things the site needs to convey.

Country Level
International
Foreign assets held abroad vs. foreign debt owed. Well-measured (IMF data). This is about globalization.
USA net position: −$15 trillion
State Level
International + Interstate
Same sovereign — "cross-border" now means where people live vs. where they work across state lines. Interstate flows are 13–399× larger than international for commuter-belt states.
County Level
Commuter Flows Dominate
A bedroom suburb vs. its downtown neighbor. Income earned in Manhattan, spent in Bergen County, NJ. This is the economic reality most Americans actually live.
This is the defining relationship at county scale
UX implication: You're building one balance-sheet page template that renders at any scale. But the narrative text and cross-border visualization must adapt — at country level it shows international flows; at county level it shows commuter dynamics. We'll provide the text for each context. You wire the template to swap it.

7. Methodology — OGs and NUSAP

Two features live under the Methodology umbrella. Neither is a headliner, but the site architecture must accommodate them from the start. Retrofitting quality metadata into a design that wasn't built for it is painful.

Operational Guidelines (OGs)

10 guidelines (OG-001 through OG-010) that codify how we handle specific methodological domains. On the site these appear as an accordion on the Standards/Methodology page: one-line summary visible, full text on expand. Institutional reviewers will read these — they signal that this is peer-grade work.

NUSAP — Data Quality Framework

NUSAP is an established framework (Funtowicz & Ravetz, 1990) for communicating data quality. Most data portals show you a number without telling you how much to trust it. We show quality metadata alongside every value. This is a differentiator: "We show you how much to trust each number."

—Stands ForWhat the User Sees
NNumeralThe value itself ($4,200 per capita)
UUnitCurrent USD, tonnes, km², etc.
SSpreadUncertainty range or confidence band
AAssessmentQualitative reliability rating (HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW)
PPedigreeFull provenance chain — which source, which tier, any statistical adjustments
How NUSAP appears on the site — progressive disclosure: The numeral (N) and unit (U) are always visible. Hover or click → see the spread (S) and assessment (A). Click "source detail" → full pedigree (P) with the referral link to the primary agency. The pedigree badges already exist in our data pipeline — we'll provide them as structured metadata you render.
