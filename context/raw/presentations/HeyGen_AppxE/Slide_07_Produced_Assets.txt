FAND's Produced Assets use OECD Source A — Table 9B, the national accounts net capital stock — for 38 OECD countries, extended via Perpetual Inventory Method to 211 countries. CWoN uses Penn World Table-based PA with its own constant-dollar treatment.

The two series agree reasonably well for advanced economies. Look at the United States, Japan, Germany panels — the black FAND line and the colored CWoN lines track each other, give or take deflator-driven divergences.

The interesting cases are the developing countries. For China, CWoN's PA estimate shows a dramatically different trajectory from FAND's — the deflator choice matters enormously when you are comparing an economy whose price structure has transformed over four decades of industrialization.

For India and Indonesia, the gap between FAND and CWoN widens over time, reflecting the compounding effect of different deflator choices over long time horizons.

The key methodological point: FAND's PA is anchored in OECD national-accounts data — the gold standard of capital stock measurement — while CWoN's is derived from PWT, which applies its own purchasing-power adjustments.
