FAND's Natural Capital is built from six independently valued branches: fossil fuels, minerals, fisheries, agricultural land, forest timber, and forest cover. Each branch has its own builder script, source registry, and pedigree tracking.

The critical difference you can see in these charts: FAND preserves market commodity prices in its stock valuations. CWoN deflates commodity prices, which — as we showed with the gold-oil ratio — destroys the relative-price signals between commodities.

For Nigeria, a major oil producer, this matters enormously. FAND's NC per capita shows the oil price booms and busts that have defined Nigeria's development trajectory. CWoN's deflated series smooths those away — presenting a misleadingly stable picture of a fundamentally volatile resource endowment.

For the advanced economies — US, Japan, Germany — NC per capita shows a long decline on a log scale, consistent with depletion and the shift toward service economies. FAND and CWoN broadly agree on the direction, but the magnitudes differ because of the deflator treatment.
