FAND's Comprehensive Wealth is computed top-down: capitalize Atlas GNI using a PMT annuity formula with demographic inputs. It is a single income anchor — one number per country-year, from which the entire balance sheet is derived by subtraction.

CWoN's total wealth is computed bottom-up: sum PA plus NC plus HC. There is no residual. CWoN normalizes Human Capital to close any gap between the sum and their total — which means HC absorbs all measurement error and model uncertainty.

This is the fundamental architectural difference. FAND's residual-bearing identity — CW equals PA plus NC plus HR plus SI — preserves SI as the unexplained remainder. SI is what institutions contribute to wealth. CWoN's approach, by eliminating the residual, eliminates the very signal that makes wealth measurement useful for governance.

The implicit deflator findings from Part 1 compound this: CWoN's constant-dollar treatment introduces relative-price distortions that ripple through all components. FAND's single deflator avoids these distortions, at the cost of admitting that "real" cross-component comparisons require more humility about what prices can tell us.
