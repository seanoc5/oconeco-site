If density and wealth were independent, every cell would show 4.0%. The departures tell a story.
The eye is drawn to the top-left (Sparse + Poor, 5.2%) and bottom-center (Dense + Lower-Middle, 6.0%). Dense places tend to be lower-middle income, not rich. Spearman correlation is negative: rho = −0.23.
Dense + Lower-Middle (Q5×Q1) shows only 1.6% — sparse places in middle-income countries like transitional zones of Brazil, Turkey, Mexico. The lowest cell at 0.9% is Dense + Upper-Middle (Q5×Q4): mainly fast-growing Chinese cities that sit in a narrow income band between Q3 and Q5. Density either traps (South Asian poverty) or amplifies (OECD metro wealth). The upper-middle density combination barely exists.
At the subnational level, crowding and prosperity are mildly opposed. The world's densest fifth is disproportionately lower-middle income, not rich. This contradicts the casual intuition that dense = prosperous (Manhattan, London) — those places exist but are statistically unusual.
