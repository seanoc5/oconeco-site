Three takeaways from this analysis.

First, prices matter more than you think. When CWoN uses component-specific deflators, it is implicitly claiming that relative prices between physical and human capital swing by factors of 7 or more. FAND's single deflator makes no such claims. This is not a minor methodological footnote — it determines whether your "real" wealth time series goes up or down in any given year.

Second, the residual is the signal. CWoN normalizes Human Capital to close its identity, which means HC absorbs all measurement error and model uncertainty. FAND's architecture preserves SI — Savings and Institutional Quality — as a measurable residual. SI is what institutions contribute to wealth. It is the most policy-relevant metric in the framework, because it captures what governance can change in the medium term. A framework that eliminates the residual eliminates the most actionable information.

Third, market prices are decision inputs. The 89% signal compression on the gold-to-oil ratio means CWoN presents a misleadingly stable picture of commodity wealth to the decision-makers who need that information most — resource-dependent countries deciding how to manage their natural endowments.
