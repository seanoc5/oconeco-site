When you use different deflators for different components, you are implicitly claiming something about relative prices. The ratio of produced-assets deflator to human-capital deflator tells you how CWoN thinks the "price" of physical capital has moved relative to human capital.

This chart shows that ratio for eight countries. FAND's ratio is 1.0 by construction — the flat blue line — because it uses the same deflator for everything. CWoN's ratio, the red shaded area, ranges from 0.15 times to 7.6 times across countries and years.

China's ratio hits 7.6 — meaning CWoN implicitly claims produced assets became seven and a half times more expensive relative to human capital. For Brazil, it ranges from 0.72 to 1.56.

The key insight: in a well-behaved price system, this ratio would be relatively stable. The wild swings mean CWoN's constant-dollar treatment is embedding large, time-varying relative-price claims into what it presents as "real" wealth comparisons. FAND's single deflator makes no such claims — it admits that relative-price movements are real economic phenomena, not measurement noise to be deflated away.
