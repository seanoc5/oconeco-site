FAND's Produced Assets use OECD Source A — Table 9B, the national accounts net capital stock — for 38 OECD countries, extended via Perpetual Inventory Method to 211 countries. CWoN uses Penn World Table-based PA with its own constant-dollar treatment.

The two series agree reasonably well for advanced economies. The United States, Japan, and Germany panels show the FAND and CWoN lines tracking each other, give or take deflator-driven divergences.

The interesting cases are the developing countries. For China, CWoN's PA estimate shows a dramatically different trajectory — the deflator choice matters enormously when comparing an economy whose price structure has transformed over four decades of industrialization.

For India and Indonesia, the gap widens over time, reflecting the compounding effect of different deflator choices over long horizons. FAND's PA is anchored in OECD national-accounts data — the gold standard of capital stock measurement — while CWoN's is derived from Penn World Tables, which apply their own purchasing-power adjustments.
